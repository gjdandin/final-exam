Echo Chamber

In Norway, 80% of the population between 16 and 79 use social media, and 53% of 9-18 year olds regularly receive news from social media (see figure. 1) (Norwegian Media Authority, 2020). For many, social media is the primary news source - what consequences does this have? Social media represent private entities with economic self-interests. This brings into question the quality of news received from these platforms. Social media wants to keep their users at their site. To this end, users are preferentially shown the content they want to see, often by sophisticated algorithms. Thus, echo chambers are formed. Polarisation between groups of users has been shown to be very high on Facebook and Twitter compared to other sites (Cinelli et al., 2021).

Users tend to join communities with ideologically-aligned peers, where their pre-existing views are strengthened. It has been shown that facebook users use fewer sources the more active they are on facebook (Schmidt et al., 2017). Often, articles supporting the group’s views are promoted whereas opposing views are looked down upon and shunned. In some cases, e.g. the anti-vaxxer movement, these groups harbor views and values far outside the norm.

These echo chambers facilitate the spread of misinformation and contribute to radicalising political discourse (Cota et al., 2019). This can affect political decision making and lead to a more polarised climate.
